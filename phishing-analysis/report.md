# Phishing Attack Scenario Analysis

## Scenario
A student receives an email that appears to be from the college IT Support Department. The subject line reads: **“Urgent: Your Student Portal Access Will Be Suspended Today.”**

The email states that unusual activity has been detected on the student’s account and that immediate verification is required to avoid losing access to the student portal, online classes, and academic records. It instructs the student to click a link labeled **“Verify Your Account Now”** and sign in using their school email address and password.

At first glance, the email looks legitimate because it uses the college name and official-style formatting. However, the sender’s email address is slightly different from the real college domain, and the message begins with a generic greeting: **“Dear Student.”** The email also creates urgency by warning that the account will be locked within 24 hours if no action is taken.

## Key Indicators of Compromise
The following signs show that this message is likely a phishing attempt:

- **Urgent language:** The email uses fear and pressure by saying the account will be suspended immediately.
- **Suspicious sender address:** The sender’s email looks similar to the college address, but the domain name is slightly misspelled.
- **Generic greeting:** The message says “Dear Student” instead of using the recipient’s real name.
- **Request for sensitive information:** The email asks the user to enter their school login credentials.
- **Suspicious link:** The verification link may lead to a fake login page created to steal usernames and passwords.
- **Social engineering tactics:** The attacker pretends to be a trusted institution to make the email look believable.

## Analysis
This scenario is a phishing attack because it uses social engineering to trick the victim into revealing confidential information. The attacker impersonates the college IT department, which is a trusted source, and creates a sense of urgency so the student reacts quickly without carefully checking the message.

Several red flags are present in the email. The suspicious sender address suggests that the message did not come from the real college domain. The generic greeting is another warning sign, since official institutions often address students by name. The biggest sign is the request for login credentials through an email link. Legitimate organizations generally do not ask users to confirm passwords this way.

If the student clicks the link and enters their username and password, the attacker could gain unauthorized access to the student portal. This may expose personal information, academic records, class materials, and other sensitive data. The attacker could also use the compromised account to send more phishing emails to other students.

## Possible Signs the Account Has Been Compromised
If the student clicked the link and entered their details, the following may indicate that the account has been compromised:

- The student can no longer log in with their normal password
- The password or recovery information has been changed without permission
- Unusual login alerts or security notifications appear
- Messages are sent from the student’s account without their knowledge
- Personal or school information is accessed or altered unexpectedly

## Recommended Response
The student should not click the link or reply to the email. Instead, they should report the message as suspicious and contact the college IT department using the official school website or contact information they already know is genuine.

If the student already clicked the link and entered their credentials, they should immediately change their password, enable multi-factor authentication if available, and notify the college IT support team so the account can be secured.

## Conclusion
This phishing scenario demonstrates how attackers use urgency, fear, and impersonation to steal sensitive information. The suspicious sender address, generic greeting, malicious link, and request for login credentials are key indicators that the message is fraudulent. Recognizing these warning signs can help users avoid phishing attacks and protect their accounts and personal information.
